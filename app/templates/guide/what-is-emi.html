What is EMI? Complete Guide to Equated Monthly Installment (2026)

Understand EMI meaning, formula, calculation method, interest types and how banks calculate EMI in India.

EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment. It is the fixed monthly payment made by a borrower to repay a loan taken from a bank or financial institution. Each EMI consists of both principal repayment and interest component.

Every EMI has two parts:

  • Principal: The original loan amount
  • Interest: The cost charged by the bank for lending money

In the initial months, a larger portion of EMI goes toward interest. Over time, the principal component increases.

Understanding EMI Structure in Depth

To fully understand EMI, it is important to break down how each monthly payment works over the life of a loan. When a borrower starts repaying a loan, the interest portion is significantly higher in the beginning. This happens because interest is calculated on the outstanding principal amount. Since the principal is highest in the early months, the interest component is also higher.

As time progresses and the borrower continues making payments, the outstanding principal reduces. Because of this reduction, the interest charged also decreases. Gradually, a larger portion of each EMI goes toward reducing the principal amount instead of interest. This shift is clearly visible in an amortization schedule.

What is an Amortization Schedule?

An amortization schedule is a detailed table that shows how each EMI is divided between principal and interest over the loan tenure. It provides transparency and helps borrowers understand exactly how much interest they are paying throughout the loan.

For example, in a 20-year home loan, the first few years mostly consist of interest payments. Only later does the principal repayment accelerate. This is why prepayment in early years saves a significant amount of interest.

Fixed vs Floating Interest Rate Loans

A fixed interest rate remains constant throughout the loan tenure. This means your EMI remains unchanged regardless of market fluctuations.

A floating interest rate changes according to RBI repo rate movements or bank benchmark rates. This can increase or decrease your EMI or loan tenure.

Floating rates are often lower initially but carry market risk. Fixed rates provide stability but may be slightly higher.

Impact of Loan Tenure on EMI

Longer tenure reduces EMI but increases total interest paid. Shorter tenure increases EMI but reduces total interest.

Borrowers must balance affordability and long-term interest savings.

EMI and Credit Score (CIBIL)

Timely EMI payments improve your credit score. Late payments negatively impact your CIBIL score and may reduce your chances of getting future loans.

Maintaining a healthy credit score above 750 helps secure better interest rates.
